## 1. The Physics of Satellite Speed: How GPS Works

To understand your speed, your phone doesn't just look at how far you've traveled over time (though that is part of it). It uses a sophisticated application of the **Doppler Effect**.

### Triangulation and Time-of-Flight
A GPS receiver works by calculating the "Time of Flight" (ToF) of signals from at least four of the 31 operational GPS satellites. By knowing the exact position of the satellites and the time the signal was sent, your phone can calculate its distance from each one. The intersection of these spheres of distance is your precise location.

### The Doppler Shift Advantage
In 2026, high-precision tools like the Calcuva **GPS Speedometer** also utilize the Doppler shift of the satellite signals. Just as a siren changes pitch as it passes you, the frequency of the GPS signal changes based on your velocity relative to the satellite. By measuring this frequency shift, your phone can calculate your instantaneous speed without waiting for a second location fix, reducing "lag" to near-zero.

## 2. Accuracy in 2026: The L1 vs. L5 Revolution

One of the biggest questions we receive at the Calcuva tech desk is: "Is my phone as accurate as my car's dashboard?" In 2026, the answer is often **yes—or even more so.**

### The "Dual-Band" Standard
Early smartphones only used the **L1 frequency**, which was prone to "multipath errors" (signals bouncing off buildings in cities). By 2026, most mid-range and flagship smartphones support **Dual-Band GPS (L1 + L5)**.
*   **L1**: The legacy signal used for basic positioning.
*   **L5**: A higher-power, more advanced signal that is much harder to "confuse" with reflections.

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### 1. Can GPS measure speed in a tunnel?
No. GPS requires a direct line-of-sight to at least 4 satellites. In a tunnel, our tool will hold the last known speed or use "Dead Reckoning" (accelerometer data) to estimate your movement until you exit.

### 2. Is GPS speed more accurate than my car's speedometer?
Usually, yes. Car manufacturers often "buffer" speedometers to read 2-5% higher than actual speed to prevent speeding liability. GPS measures your true ground speed.
