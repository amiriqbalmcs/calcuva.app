The United States uses a **Progressive Tax System**. This means that as you earn more, you contribute a higher percentage of your *incremental* earnings to the government. Understanding the tiers helps you make better decisions about retirement contributions and salary negotiations.

## Marginal vs. Effective Rates

### Marginal Tax Rate
This is the rate applied to the very last dollar you earned. If you are in the "22% Bracket," it only means that your highest earners are taxed at that rate, not your entire salary.

### Effective Tax Rate
This is your "Real" tax rate. Because of lower brackets and the standard deduction, someone in the 22% marginal bracket might only have an effective rate of 14% or 15%.

## Example: The "Raise" Math

Imagine the bracket jumps from 12% to 22% at $47,150. If you earn $47,151, only **one dollar** is taxed at 22%. The rest of your income is still taxed at the 10% and 12% rates. **It is mathematically impossible for a raise to reduce your take-home pay.**

## Reducing Your Taxable Income

You can lower the amount of income the government "sees" by using:
- **401(k) or Traditional IRA Contributions**: These are "Pre-Tax," meaning they are subtracted from your gross income before brackets are applied.
- **HSA Contributions**: Health Savings Accounts are another powerful way to reduce your taxable footprint.
- **Itemized Deductions**: If your specific expenses (like mortgage interest and charity) exceed the Standard Deduction, you can itemize to lower your tax bill further.
