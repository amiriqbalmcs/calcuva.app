#### Understanding the 4.0 Scale (The US Standard)
The 4.0 scale is the most common system in North America and international schools.
*   **4.0 (A)**: Excellent
*   **3.0 (B)**: Good
*   **2.0 (C)**: Satisfactory
*   **1.0 (D)**: Poor
Because this scale is so compressed, a single 'C' grade can drastically pull down your average. This creates the "Weighted" system, where an 'A' in a difficult subject counts as a 5.0, helping to "protect" the overall GPA from the impact of challenging coursework.

#### The 10.0 Scale and the "Magic" 9.5 Multiplier
The 10.0 CGPA system (common in India, Europe, and some Middle Eastern regions) is more granular.
**The Conversion Trap**: Many students simply multiply by 10 (e.g., 8.0 = 80%). However, most official boards require a **9.5 multiplier**. 
*   **Reasoning**: Statistical analysis of student performance showed that a student with an 8.0 CGPA actually performs at the level of a student who scored 76% in the traditional raw-mark system. Using the correct 9.5 multiplier is critical for ensuring your resume doesn't get rejected by automated screening filters (ATS).

#### Technical Component: The Weighted Average Formula
Your final GPA/Percentage is not a simple average; it is a **Weighted Average** based on credits.
`Final GPA = Σ (Grade Points x Credits) / Σ (Total Credits)`
If you score an 'A' (4 points) in a 4-credit course and a 'C' (2 points) in a 1-credit lab, your GPA isn't (4+2)/2 = 3.0. 
It is: `[(4x4) + (2x1)] / 5 = 18 / 5 = 3.6`.
