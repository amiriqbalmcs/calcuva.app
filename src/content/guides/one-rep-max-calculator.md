Your One-Rep Max (1RM) is the maximum amount of weight you can lift for a single repetition of a given exercise with proper form. It is the gold standard for measuring absolute strength in movements like the squat, bench press, and deadlift.

## The Science of Estimation: Why We Use Math Instead of Ego

Testing a true 1RM is taxing on the Central Nervous System (CNS) and requires significant recovery time. When you attempt a 100% effort lift, your brain sends an intense electrical signal to your muscle fibers. This "Neurological Exhaustion" can take 48-72 hours to recover from, even if your muscles don't feel sore. Because of this, most lifters in 2026 use mathematical models to predict their 1RM based on "reps to failure" with lighter weights.

### The Epley Formula
Developed by Boyd Epley in 1985:  
**1RM = w * (1 + r / 30)**  
*(where w is weight and r is reps)*

### The Brzycki Formula
Developed by Matt Brzycki:  
**1RM = w / (1.0278 - (0.0278 * r))**

### Which one should you use?
*   **The Epley Formula** is generally considered more accurate for reps between 5 and 10.
*   **The Brzycki Formula** is often the favorite of powerlifters working in the 2-5 rep range.
*   **The Calcuva Approach**: Our calculator provides an average or allows you to see the range, giving you a more conservative and safer estimate to work with.

## Training with Percentages: The 2026 Programming Standard

Once you know your 1RM, you can structure your training sessions using percentages of that max to achieve specific goals. This is called **Percentage-Based Training (PBT)**.

- **Absolute Strength (85% – 100% of 1RM)**: Used for peaking before a competition. Very high intensity, low volume.
- **Hypertrophy/Muscle Growth (65% – 85% of 1RM)**: The "Sweet Spot" for most gym-goers. Balancing tension and metabolic stress.
- **Power and Speed (50% – 60% of 1RM)**: Focused on moving the weight as fast as possible. Essential for athletes (sprinters, fighters).
- **Muscular Endurance (< 50% of 1RM)**: High repetitions (15+) to improve blood flow and mitochondrial density.

## The RPE Scale: The Qualitative Backstop

In 2026, many coaches use the **Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)** alongside 1RM.
*   **RPE 10**: Maximum effort (0 reps left in the tank).
*   **RPE 9**: Heavy (1 rep left in the tank).
*   **RPE 8**: Solid (2 reps left).
*   **The Math**: An RPE 8 set of 5 reps corresponds roughly to 80% of your 1RM. If your estimated 1RM says you should lift 100kg for 5, but at 90kg you feel like you're at RPE 10, your "Daily 1RM" is lower due to fatigue. **Listen to your body, not just the calculator.**

## Safety Protocols for 1RM Testing

If you decide to test your true 1RM (instead of estimating):
1.  **Warm-up is Mandatory**: Do not jump into a 1RM. Use a "Ramping" set: 5 reps @ 50%, 3 reps @ 70%, 1 rep @ 90%, then the attempt.
2.  **The Spotter Rule**: Never attempt a 1RM on the bench press or squat without a competent spotter or safety bars (pins) in a power rack.
3.  **Form Over Weight**: If your lower back rounds during a deadlift or your knees cave in during a squat, the rep doesn't count. A "Dirty Rep" 1RM is a recipe for a 6-month injury.

## General Strength Standards (2026 Benchmarks)

What is a "Good" 1RM? For a standard 80kg male:
*   **Beginner**: Bench 60kg, Squat 80kg, Deadlift 100kg.
*   **Intermediate**: Bench 1.0x Bodyweight, Squat 1.5x Bodyweight, Deadlift 1.75x Bodyweight.
*   **Advanced**: Bench 1.5x Bodyweight, Squat 2.0x Bodyweight, Deadlift 2.5x Bodyweight.

## Important Limitation

Strength estimation becomes significantly less accurate as repetitions increase. A 3-rep max estimation is very reliable, while a 15-rep max estimation has a high margin of error because cardiovascular endurance and lactic acid buildup become limiting factors before absolute muscular strength.
