### The Bedrock of Financial Safety
A **Fixed Deposit (FD)**, also known as a Term Deposit, is the "safe haven" of the financial world. While the stock market fluctuates, an FD provides a contractual guarantee: you know exactly how much you will have on the **Maturity Date**. For conservative investors or those saving for a short-term goal (like a wedding or a house down payment), the FD is an indispensable tool.

#### Cumulative Interest: The Reinvestment Secret
Most professional investors choose the **Cumulative** option. Here, the interest is calculated quarterly and added back to your principal. 
*   **Result**: You earn interest on your interest. 
*   **Comparison**: A $10,000 FD at 7% for 5 years earns **$3,500** in simple interest, but **$4,147** in cumulative (compounded) interest. That $647 difference is pure "free money" earned just by choosing the right reinvestment option.

#### Expert Strategy: The FD Laddering Masterclass
The biggest downside of an FD is **Lock-in**. If you put all your cash into a 5-year FD and have an emergency in Year 2, you have to break the FD and pay a penalty.
**The Solution: Laddering.**
Divide $50,000 into five $10,000 FDs:
*   FD 1: 1-Year Tenure
*   FD 2: 2-Year Tenure
*   FD 3: 3-Year Tenure
*   FD 4: 4-Year Tenure
*   FD 5: 5-Year Tenure
**Every year**, one FD will mature. You can either use the cash or reinvest it for another 5 years. This ensures you are never more than 12 months away from liquidity while still capturing the high 5-year interest rates.

#### Technical Component: Simple vs. Compound Formulas
Our calculator uses the two standard industry formulas:
1. **Simple Interest**: `A = P(1 + rt)`
2. **Compound Interest (Quarterly)**: `A = P(1 + r/4)^(4t)`
*   **A**: Maturity amount
*   **P**: Principal
*   **r**: Annual interest rate
*   **t**: Number of years

#### The Tax Trap (TDS & Slabs)
One of the "hidden costs" of an FD is the tax liability. Unlike long-term stocks (which may have preferential tax rates), FD interest is usually taxed as regular income. If you are in a **30% Tax Bracket**, a 7% FD is actually only a **4.9% FD** after taxes. Always use Calcuva to calculate your "Post-Tax Return" before comparing an FD to other investment vehicles.

#### Case Study: FD vs. Inflation
In high-inflation environments, an FD can actually lose you money in "Real Terms."
*   **FD Rate**: 6%
*   **Inflation**: 7%
*   **Result**: You gain 6% more currency, but everything you want to buy costs 7% more. You have effectively lost 1% of your purchasing power. For this reason, we recommend FDs for **Capital Protection**, not long-term wealth growth (which is better handled via an [SIP](/calculators/sip-investment-calculator)).

#### When to Use an FD
1. **Emergency Fund**: Keep 3–6 months of expenses in a liquid FD.
2. **Risk Mitigation**: Balance a high-risk crypto or stock portfolio.
3. **Short-Term Goals**: Saving for a purchase within the next 3 years.
4. **Retirement Income**: A non-cumulative FD providing monthly payouts for seniors.
