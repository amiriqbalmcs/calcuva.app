Fat loss is fundamentally driven by thermodynamics: energy in versus energy out. While the quality of your food (macros) affects your energy, hormones, and muscle retention, the absolute quantity of food (calories) dictates whether you lose or gain weight.

## Your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)

Before calculating a deficit, you must know your maintenance calories, or **TDEE**. This number consists of:
1. **BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate):** The calories your body burns just staying alive in a coma (breathing, organ function). This is usually 60-70% of your total calories.
2. **TEF (Thermic Effect of Food):** The energy used to digest food (about 10%).
3. **NEAT & EAT (Activity):** The energy burned through daily movements, walking, fidgeting, and dedicated exercise.

## Setting Your Deficit Pace

Once you know your TDEE, you subtract calories to force weight loss. 

- **Mild (-250 kcal/day):** Loses about 0.5 lbs (0.25 kg) per week. Excellent for extreme athletes trying to preserve maximum muscle.
- **Normal (-500 kcal/day):** Loses about 1.0 lbs (0.5 kg) per week. The standard, highly sustainable pace recommended by doctors.
- **Fast (-750 kcal/day):** Loses about 1.5 lbs (0.75 kg) per week. Requires strict tracking and high protein intake to prevent muscle loss.
- **Extreme (-1000 kcal/day):** Loses about 2.0 lbs (1.0 kg) per week. Difficult to sustain long-term and increases risk of binge eating.

## Re-calculating as You Lose

As you lose weight, a smaller body requires fewer calories to maintain itself. This means your TDEE will slowly drop. If you hit a "plateau", it usually means your new TDEE has dropped to match your current intake, eliminating your deficit. Re-calculate your numbers every 5-10 lbs (2-5 kg) lost.
