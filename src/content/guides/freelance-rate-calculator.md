Transitioning from a salary to a freelance rate is a common pitfall for new consultants. Many simply divide their annual salary by 2,080 (the number of working hours in a year), but this leads to significant under-earning.

## The Freelancer's Tax & Benefit Load

When you are self-employed, you are responsible for both the employer and employee portions of social security and medicare taxes. You also lose access to employer-sponsored health insurance and paid vacation. To maintain the same lifestyle as a $60,000 employee, you typically need to generate closer to $90,000 in gross revenue.

## Calculating Your "Survival Floor"

Your minimum rate should be calculated as:  
**Total Annual Costs / Total Annual Billable Hours**

Where Total Annual Costs = (Personal Expenses + Business Expenses + Taxes + Savings).

## Value-Based Pricing vs. Hourly Rates

While an hourly rate is a great baseline, once you become an expert, you should consider **Value-Based Pricing**. This involves charging based on the result you provide the client rather than the time it takes you to do it. If you can save a company $100,000 in two hours, you shouldn't just charge for two hours of your time.
