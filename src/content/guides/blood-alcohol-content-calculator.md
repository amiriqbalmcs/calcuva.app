### The Architecture of Impairment
Blood Alcohol Content (BAC) is a measure of the mass of alcohol per unit volume of blood. While it's often discussed in a social context, the physics of alcohol metabolism is a serious clinical matter. Understanding your BAC is not about "gaming the system"; it's about understanding the **Biological Clearance Rate** of your body to make informed safety decisions. Calcuva uses the **Widmark Formula**, the global forensic standard for BAC estimation.

#### The Widmark Formula: Measuring the Burn
To estimate your BAC, we look at four variables:
1. **Alcohol Consumed (A)**: The total grams of ethanol.
2. **Body Weight (W)**: Larger bodies have more water to dilute the alcohol.
3. **Gender (r)**: The "Volume of Distribution" constant (0.68 for men, 0.55 for women).
4. **Time (T)**: How long your liver has been working on the alcohol.
**Formula**: `BAC = [ (A / (W x r)) x 100 ] - (1.5 x T)`

#### Expert Strategy: The "Safety Window" Rule
A common error is assuming you are safe once you "feel fine." Impairment (slower reaction time, poor depth perception) begins at **0.02%**, long before the legal 0.08% limit. 
**The Rule**: If your BAC is 0.08%, you need approximately **5.3 hours** of total rest before your blood is clear of alcohol. Use our [BAC Calculator] to find your "Zero-Point" time before you even consider getting behind the wheel or operating machinery.

#### Case Study: The "Next Morning" Trap
Many people are "Legally Impaired" the morning after a heavy night of drinking.
*   **12:00 AM**: BAC is 0.16% (double the limit).
*   **8:00 AM**: After 8 hours of sleep, the body has cleared 0.12% (8 x 0.015).
*   **Current BAC**: **0.04%**.
While you feel "rested," your reaction time is still technically impaired. For safety-sensitive jobs (pilots, surgeons, drivers), knowing this "Residual BAC" is a professional requirement.

#### Technical Component: The Rate of Elimination (β)
Your liver is a "Constant Rate" machine. Unlike many other drugs, alcohol follows **Zero-Order Kinetics**. This means your liver processes about **0.015% per hour** regardless of how much you have had to drink. You cannot "speed up" this process with cold showers, exercise, or "sweating it out." Your body's metabolism is a fixed biological timer.

#### Biological Nuance: The Full Stomach Effect
Food in the stomach slows the **rate of absorption** but not the total amount absorbed. If you drink on an empty stomach, your BAC will spike rapidly (reaching peak in 30 minutes). If you eat a large meal, the peak may take 90 minutes. This creates a "Delay" in impairment that can be dangerous if you don't account for it in your [Safety Planning].

#### The Impact on Sleep and Recovery
Even a low BAC (0.02%) disrupts the **REM Cycle** of sleep. This is why you feel exhausted after "drinking to sleep." Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant that prevents the brain from entering the restorative phases of sleep.
