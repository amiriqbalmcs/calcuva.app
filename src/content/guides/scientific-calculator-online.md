#### The Hierarchy of Operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS)
The most common error in mathematics is the **Order of Operations**. Without a standardized system, the problem `2 + 3 x 4` could be 20 or 14.
*   **The Rule**: We always multiply before we add.
*   **The Result**: 14.

#### Expert Strategy: Mastering Trigonometry (Sin, Cos, Tan)
Trigonometric functions are the language of circles and waves.
1. **Sine (Sin)**: The ratio of the Opposite side to the Hypotenuse.
2. **Cosine (Cos)**: The ratio of the Adjacent side to the Hypotenuse.
3. **Tangent (Tan)**: The ratio of Opposite to Adjacent.
**Pro Tip**: When using these for construction (e.g., calculating the pitch of a roof), ensure your calculator is in **DEG (Degree) mode**. If you are doing theoretical physics or advanced calculus, switch to **RAD (Radian) mode** to avoid being off by a factor of ~57.3.

#### Case Study: The Power of Logarithms
Logarithms allow us to represent massive exponential scales in manageable linear chunks.
*   **Decibels (Sound)**: A 10dB increase means the sound is 10x more powerful. A 20dB increase is 100x more powerful.
*   **Earthquakes (Richter Scale)**: A 7.0 quake is 10x stronger than a 6.0 quake.

#### Technical Component: Transcendental Numbers (PI and e)
A professional scientific calculator must provide constant access to irrational numbers.
*   **π (Pi, ~3.14159)**: The ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. It appears in everything from satellite orbits to wave mechanics.
*   **e (Euler's Number, ~2.71828)**: The "Natural Base" of growth. It is the fundamental constant of continuous compounding in finance and population biology.
Our tool stores these to 15 decimal places to ensure that cumulative rounding errors don't degrade the integrity of your long-form calculations.

#### Memory Management (M+, MR, MC)
Advanced users don't write down intermediate results. They use the **Memory Stack**.
*   **M+**: Adds the current result to the "hidden shelf."
*   **MR (Memory Recall)**: Pulls the total off the shelf.
*   **MC (Memory Clear)**: Wipes the shelf clean.
By keeping partial totals in memory, you eliminate "Transcription Errors"—the most frequent cause of mistakes in engineering exams and professional reports.
